Summary

Our Animals, Food and Technology survey tracks attitudes towards animal farming and animal-free food in the US over time. In 2020, as in previous years, we found significant opposition to various aspects of the animal farming industry, with a majority of people reporting discomfort with the industry, and strong support for a range of often quite radical policy changes, such as banning slaughterhouses. While the trend in attitudes between 2017 and 2020 has been relatively stable, there were more negative than positive changes. Notably, the number of people who consider animal farming to be one of the most important social issues fell over the period.

Analysis of the relationships between people’s attitudes (measured by animal farming opposition and animal product alternatives support) and various demographic variables revealed that age, politics, and diet were associated with both measures. There were regional and ethnicity differences, which are plausibly explained by average differences in political views. While females were more opposed to animal farming, we found no sex differences in animal product alternative support. Education and income were positively associated with animal product alternative support but not with animal farming opposition.

Introduction

In October 2020, we conducted the third iteration of the Animals, Food and Technology (AFT) survey on attitudes towards animal farming and animal-free food with 1,113 US adults, census-balanced to be representative of age, sex, region, ethnicity, and income. Here we report:

  1. The results from the 2020 survey.
  2. The trends in responses between 2017 and 2020.
  3. The results of regressions of respondents’ opposition to animal farming and support for animal product alternatives (i.e., plant-based and cultured meat products) on a range of demographic variables.

Our methodology remained largely the same as in previous years and can be found in our 2017 report. Any deviations from this are reported in the text. Links to the data, survey, and analysis code for the results presented in this report can be found here.

2020 Results

Figure 1 presents the means and distributions of responses for the questions in the 2020 survey, weighted to account for differences between our sample and the US population on various demographic characteristics as in previous iterations of the survey.

Figure 1:  Animals, Food and Technology survey 2020 weighted means and distributions. Please use the dropdown menu to show results for questions of interest.

The results for 2020 are broadly consistent with those of previous years:

  • Positively, a majority of people, 66.7%, reported feeling some discomfort with the animal farming industry, and 83.9% of people think animals have the roughly the same capacity to experience pain as humans. 43.2% consider animal farming to be one of the most important social issues in the world today.
  • People’s positive attitudes translate into support for various policies: 45.4% of people are in favor of banning factory farming, 39.5% are in favor of banning slaughterhouses, and 31.9% are in favor of banning all animal farming completely.
  • However, while people on average believe that 68.5% of animals come from factory farms, a surprisingly high number reported that they think farmed animals are treated well (56.2%), and an even larger number (65.4%) reported that their usual sources of animal products treat animals humanely.
  • Relatively large proportions of respondents reported that they think people should consume fewer animal products (57.4%), and that they themselves are trying to reduce their animal product consumption (48.2%). However, 92.9% still think that eating animals is a personal choice.

2017–2020 Trends

Figure 2 presents trends in the weighted mean responses to each survey question for the three years we have collected data. The chart also shows the trends in the two scales we developed from these questions: Animal Farming Opposition (AFO) and Animal Product Alternatives Support (APAS). Definitions of these and all other questions in the survey can be found here.

Figure 2:  Animals, Food and Technology survey 2017–2020 trends. Please click on items on the legend to show trends for questions of interest.

Overall, the charts suggest that people’s responses have been relatively stable over the period. Statistical analysis of time trends supports this: there was no significant change in APAS over the period, and while there was an increase in animal farming opposition between 2017 and 2019, the difference between 2017 and 2020 was not significant. The full results can be found here. We report the key findings below.

  • Between 2017 and 2019, there was a significant reduction in the proportion of respondents who thought that animal farming is one of the most important social issues, and this change persisted through 2020.
  • There was increased discomfort with the animal farming industry between 2017 and 2019, however, in 2020 this fell back to the 2017 level. The reversed question showed that the average reported level of comfort with the industry was greater in 2020 compared to 2019. Between 2019 and 2020, there was a significant increase in the number of people agreeing that farmed animals are treated humanely.
  • There was also a significant increase in the extent to which people consider that the animal products that they themselves buy come from animals that are treated humanely. There was a decrease in the proportion of respondents who reported that they are trying to reduce their animal product consumption in both 2019 and 2020. People also felt less obliged to do so once prices are the same in both years.
  • Compared to 2017, there was less agreement that people should eat more plant-based products. There was also a significant reduction in the proportion of respondents who think that people should eat more plant-based foods even when prices are the same.
  • On the positive side, people on average said they would donate a greater proportion of $10 to animal causes if they were given the chance, in both 2019 and 2020. There was also a decrease in the extent to which people think that eating animals is a personal choice between 2019 and 2020.

The analysis revealed several notable relationships:

  • Age was significantly negatively correlated with both animal farming opposition and animal product alternative support across all the models.
  • Sex was significantly correlated with animal farming opposition, with males reporting reduced opposition. However, it was not correlated with animal product alternative support.
  • The Northeast region was associated with greater animal farming opposition than the South. However, after including political and diet variables, this relationship was no longer significant, suggesting it may be driven by these factors. In one of the models, we found that the West was associated with lower animal product alternatives support than the South.
  • Being Black and Hispanic was associated with greater animal farming opposition and greater support for animal product alternatives in the pooled models. In the 2020 models, after including the political and diet variables, these associations became insignificant.
  • While there were no differences in AFO between those with and without a college degree, there was a significant positive association between having a college degree and support for animal product alternatives.
  • Similarly, there was no relationship between income and animal farming opposition, though there was a relationship between income and APAS (though the effect size was relatively small).
  • Political conservatism was associated with lower animal farming opposition and lower support for animal product alternatives. Relative to Democrats, and holding conservatism constant, Republicans reported both lower AFO and APAS. Being an Independent was associated with lower APAS but not lower AFO.
  • Unsurprisingly, being vegan or vegetarian was the strongest predictor of both animal farming opposition and animal product alternatives support.
